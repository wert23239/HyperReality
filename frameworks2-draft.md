## Page: A Performative Checkers Player

I play checkers. You play chess. That's what you think.

I show up to every event. I text back too fast. I plan the group trip that no one asked for. I remember your birthday and your dog's name and the thing you said three months ago about wanting to try that restaurant in the East Village.

And because I do all of this, you assume I'm simple. That I'm the nice guy. The easy one. The one who doesn't keep score.

But I keep score.

I mark every time you flake. I mark every time you show up 40 minutes late and don't apologize. I mark every time you only text me when you need something. I file every single interaction into a system you will never see.

The difference between checkers and chess isn't complexity. It's that in checkers, all the pieces look the same. You can't tell which one is about to king. You don't know which piece has been quietly moving diagonal across the whole board while you were focused on the center.

I'm not naive. I'm patient. Those are not the same thing.

When I finally cut someone off — when I go cold and they're confused — I want them to know it wasn't sudden. It was the longest, most calculated decision I've ever made. I considered every alternative. I replayed every moment. And I still chose to walk away.

That's not checkers. That's not even chess. That's counting cards at a table where everyone else thinks we're playing Go Fish.

## Page: Early Onset Alzheimer's

I have a condition. I forget everything you've done to me.

Not the Alzheimer's where you lose your keys or forget your mom's face. The kind where someone wrongs you and your brain just... files it under "whatever." The kind where you wake up the next morning and the anger is gone and you're already texting them like nothing happened.

People think forgiveness is noble. It's not. It's a disease. It's a deficiency in the part of your brain that's supposed to protect you. Normal people build walls after someone hurts them. I build a welcome mat.

Here's what early onset Alzheimer's looks like in practice: Someone talks shit about you at a party. You hear about it two days later. You're pissed for about four hours. Then you see them at the next party and your brain goes, "Oh they're actually kind of fun," and resets the whole scoreboard.

The worst part? You know you're doing it. You're watching yourself forgive in real time and you're screaming at yourself to stop. But you can't. Because your default setting is trust, and no amount of evidence has been able to change that firmware.

Every "I swear I'm done with that person" has an expiration date of about 72 hours.

Some people hold grudges like trophies. I can't even hold one long enough to learn the lesson.

## Page: A Kalshi Bet

What if you could bet real money on someone being toxic?

Like, actually open your phone, go to Kalshi, and place $50 on "She will give me an ultimatum before March." Or "He will ghost after the third date." Or "My roommate will leave passive-aggressive notes instead of just talking to me."

The prediction market for human behavior already exists — it's called experience. You already know what's going to happen. You've seen the pattern before. You know exactly how this plays out.

The issue is you bet on the wrong side every time.

You know she's going to give you the ultimatum. You can feel it building like pressure in your ears on a plane. But instead of hedging — instead of having the conversation early — you just let it happen. You watch the clock tick down on a bet you already know you're losing.

Toxic ultimatums aren't surprises. They're scheduled events. The "it's me or your friends" was always coming. The "if you don't do X by Y then I'm done" was written in the terms and conditions you agreed to on date three.

The real gamble isn't whether the ultimatum comes. It's whether you'll fold or call. And most people fold. Not because the other person is right, but because folding is easier than finding out what happens when you say no.

I'd bet real money on that.

## Page: The 25% No Challenge

I said yes to everything for years. Every party. Every favor. Every "can you help me move?" Every "you should come out tonight." Every single thing anyone ever asked me.

And it almost killed me.

Not literally. But the version of me that existed before the 25% rule was a person running on empty who smiled while doing it. A person so afraid of missing out or letting someone down that he forgot he was also someone who could be let down.

The rule is simple: Say no to 25% of things. One out of every four requests, invitations, obligations — just say no. No excuse. No rain check. Just no.

It sounds easy. It's not. Because when you're wired to say yes, every no feels like you're letting someone die. Your brain screams that this is the one time it actually matters, that this is the exception, that you can rest later.

But here's what no one tells you about saying no: people respect you more. Not less. More. Because a yes from someone who says yes to everything is worthless. It's the default. It's expected. But a yes from someone who sometimes says no? That means something.

The 25% isn't about being selfish. It's about making your yes actually mean something.

## Page: A Good Boy

I am the best employee you've ever had. I show up on time. I do exactly what you ask. I hit every deadline. I never complain.

I am also doing the absolute bare minimum, and you will never know the difference.

This is malicious compliance. The quiet rebellion of the overachiever who got tired. You want a report by Friday? Here's your report by Friday. Exactly what you asked for. Nothing more. No extra insight. No initiative. No "I also noticed this other thing that might help." Just the report.

You wanted a good boy? Here's your good boy.

The thing about being someone who always goes above and beyond is that "above and beyond" becomes your baseline. People don't see the extra effort — they see the normal. So when you finally scale back to what's actually required, no one notices. They just think you're still killing it.

Malicious compliance isn't laziness. It's the strategic withdrawal of the effort that was never appreciated in the first place. It's the realization that giving 150% and giving 100% produce the exact same reaction from everyone around you.

So why would I give you 150? You didn't even notice the last 50.

## Page: Contempt Problem

There's a word for the thing that kills relationships, and it's not cheating or distance or money. It's contempt.

Contempt is the eye roll when they talk. The sigh before you respond. The way you describe them to your friends with that little edge in your voice. It's not hatred — hatred is honest. Contempt is the thing that wears a smile while it poisons everything.

John Gottman — the psychologist who can predict divorce with 94% accuracy — says contempt is the number one killer. Not conflict. Not even betrayal. Contempt. Because conflict means you still care enough to fight. Contempt means you've already decided they're beneath you.

And here's the fucked up part: contempt grows in the people who care the most. Because you have to have once respected someone to feel contempt for them. Strangers don't get your contempt — they get your indifference. Contempt is reserved for the people you loved.

I've felt it. That moment where someone you used to admire does something small — leaves their dishes out, says something slightly dumb — and instead of brushing it off, your brain files it as evidence. Exhibit 47 in the case of why this person isn't who you thought they were.

Once you start collecting evidence, it's over. You can't uncollect it. Every small thing becomes proof of the larger truth you've already decided.

The only cure is catching it early. The first eye roll. The first internal "of course they did." That's the alarm. If you ignore it, you're already dead. You just don't know it yet.

## Page: Fifth Morale Friends

You are the average of the five people you spend the most time with. Everyone knows that quote. No one actually applies it.

Here's what it actually means: you can only really hang out with people who match your morale. Not your vibe. Not your interests. Your morale.

Morale is the thing underneath everything else. It's whether someone walks into a room charged up or drained. Whether they add energy or subtract it. Whether being around them makes you feel like you can conquer the world or like you need a nap.

Low-morale friends aren't bad people. They're just people going through it. And going through it is contagious. You sit across from someone who hates their job, hates their apartment, hates their ex, and by the end of dinner you're questioning your own shit too. Their gravity pulls you down.

High-morale friends are the opposite drug. They make you feel like your ideas are possible. Like the trip is worth booking. Like the project is worth starting. Not because they're delusional — because they're charged.

The hard truth is that friendships have a morale threshold. Below a certain point, hanging out stops being fun and starts being volunteering. And you can only volunteer for so long before you run out.

I'm not saying cut people off when they're struggling. I'm saying know the difference between someone going through a rough patch and someone who lives there permanently. One you can visit. The other will make you a resident.

## Page: I Just Want to Be Met

I'm not DTF. I'm not playing checkers. I'm not trying to network. I'm not looking for a therapist or a project or a charity case.

I just want to be met.

That's it. That's the whole thing. Meet me where I am. Not where you think I should be. Not where I was six months ago. Not where you need me to be for your narrative to make sense.

The amount of relationships — friendships, romantic, professional — that die because two people refuse to meet each other where they actually are is insane. Instead, everyone's trying to pull the other person to their position. Come to my level. See it my way. Want what I want.

Meeting someone isn't agreement. It's acknowledgment. It's "I see where you are, and I'm standing here with you, even if I think you should be somewhere else."

Most people have never been met. They've been tolerated. They've been managed. They've been strategized around. But met? Seen for exactly who they are in this exact moment without an agenda attached?

Almost never.

And the fucked up thing is, it's free. It costs nothing. It's literally just showing up without a plan. But people would rather spend three hours crafting the perfect text than just say, "Hey, I see you."

That's all I want. And somehow, in a city of 8 million people, it's the hardest thing to find.

## Page: The Great Filter

In space, there's a theory called the Great Filter. It explains why we haven't found aliens yet. Somewhere between dead rock and spacefaring civilization, there's a wall — a filter so brutal that almost nothing gets through.

People have the same filter.

Somewhere between "I have potential" and "I actually did the thing," there's a wall. And almost no one gets past it. Not because they're not talented. Not because they don't have ideas. But because the filter isn't about talent or ideas. It's about the boring, ugly, unsexy middle part where nothing is working and nobody cares yet.

The filter is month four of the project when the excitement is gone. It's the twentieth rejection email. It's the moment your friends stop asking how the thing is going because they've already mentally filed it under "that phase."

Most people turn back at the filter. They pivot. They start something new. They rebrand the quitting as "growth" or "self-care" or "realizing it wasn't for me." And sometimes that's true. But most of the time, the filter just won.

The people who make it through aren't special. They're just stubborn in the specific way that matters. They kept going not because they believed it would work, but because they couldn't live with the alternative.

The Great Filter isn't talent. It's tolerance for looking stupid for longer than most people can handle.

## Page: Collateral Damage

Nobody talks about the collateral damage of ambition.

When you chase something hard — a book, a company, a dream, whatever — you become a different kind of selfish. Not the evil kind. The focused kind. The kind where Friday nights become work sessions and phone calls go unanswered and friends slowly realize you're not ignoring them, you're just somewhere else entirely.

And they're right to be upset. Because from their perspective, you chose the thing over them. And in a way, you did.

The hardest part of building something is the people who get caught in the wake. The girlfriend who wanted a present boyfriend, not a guy with a "vision." The friend who just wanted to grab dinner, not hear about the project again. The family member who doesn't understand why you can't just be normal for five seconds.

Collateral damage isn't intentional. That's what makes it so insidious. You're not trying to hurt anyone. You're just trying to build something. But building requires material, and sometimes the material is time and attention that used to belong to someone else.

I don't have a solution. Every ambitious person carries a body count of neglected relationships they didn't mean to neglect. The only honest thing to do is acknowledge it. Not justify it. Not pretend the mission was worth every sacrifice. Just say: "Yeah. Some people got hurt on the way here. And I wish they hadn't."

That's not an apology. It's an inventory. And it's the least you owe the people standing in your blast radius.
